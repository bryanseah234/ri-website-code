The Nurturers

For some, nurturing children is second nature. This can be said for some parents of Rafflesian alumni, who, taking pride in caring for others beyond family and strengthened by the tightknit friendships formed between parents throughout their children’s years in RI, got together to form the Raffles Parents’ Association Alumni (RPAA) in January 2013. An informal gathering of parents of alumni, they have been continuously supporting the events and activities organized by the Raffles Parents’ Association (RPA). Besides nurturing current Rafflesians, and supporting current and potential parents of RI together with RPA, the members also wanted RPAA to be an avenue for all who care about the regional community. 

Sometime in July, while RI staff and students were busy with Raffles Homecoming and the launch of the Raffles Community Initiative (RCI), parents from both RPA and RPAA started brewing ideas for their own community initiative. A decision was made to carry out their initiative overseas, where the parents could help the community and have a short getaway too. 

Yangon Girls’ Training School, Myanmar

Eventually, all agreed on the refurbishment of Yangon Girls’ Training School, a humble government girls’ school in Myanmar that had an enrolment of 250 students in total. The location and government school was chosen for two reasons – the first being that the chairman of RPAA, Mr Chua Wah Ann, had Myanmar employees who could easily liaise with the contractors and supervise the refurbishment that was projected to take three months, and the second being that like RI, the school of choice had to be a government-aided school.

The state of the government school before it was refurbished.

The school received a fresh coat of paint, replacement of rotting wood and much more. 

‘It was the first project we came up with since RCI was launched. We were raring to go – we started to raise funds by any means that we had. Marilyn (RPAA member) had contacts to sell at a flea market in Kebun Baru and we jumped on that opportunity to raise some funds. All of us went around talking to our family and friends about our initiative and shared our idea to raise money for the school. 

Many of us could not wait to actually fly to Myanmar to carry out the activities, especially whenever we receive photos of the progress of the refurbishment of the school from my Myanmar staff’s family members! Our excitement fuelled us to continuously seek out more monetary and non-monetary help to ensure that we did our best to help the school.

RPA and RPA alumni members sold all kinds of knick knacks at the flea market!

Besides raising enough funds to refurbish the school, the team of thirty-three also went about collecting second-hand books from family and friends. Towards the end of November, the team received a phone call from Penguin Books Singapore, who agreed to donate a substantial pile of new books for the school!

The members happily packed them for the trip, reveling in the beauty of the books and proud of how it would impact the lives of the students in the future.

When the team finally arrived in Myanmar on 6 December 2013, they were able to add the finishing touches and officially launch the refurbished school. After a warm welcome by the school principal and the students, the group took to task the unpacking of their cartons of boxes and decorating the interior of the school.

Unpacking all the boxes and furnishing the school - how many hands do you need to hang up the curtains?

Soon after, everyone proceeded to the entrance of the school to witness the official launch of the refurbishment, followed by a graceful display of traditional dance put up by the students themselves.

The official launch of the refurbished school was graced by the principal of Yangon Girls' Training school, former Principal of RI, and Chairman of RPA Alumni.

The students performed many different dances, portraying the beauty of the culture.

At the end of their week in Myanmar, the team of thirty-three was filled with pride for being able to make a significant impact on the lives of the students and the village. 

‘We’re really glad that we were able to make a huge difference to the girls’ education although we only had a short period of time to manage the project from start to finish. There’s nothing more satisfying than to be able to empower children through education. It is only right that the place where they spend half their waking hours to learn and grow is as beautiful as their bright young minds,’ shared Mr Richard Wong, current Chairman of RPA, after returning back to Singapore.

Helping our neighbours

When asked why they would rather take leave from work to be involved in communal efforts overseas than prioritise a holiday, Mr Chua explained the two key factors that guided their decision. 

‘As parents, we respect and admire our big hearted children who would like to help beyond their immediate community. Yet we worry about their safety, and we want them to be able to leave a huge impact rather than be constrained by costs and time. We realized that instead of merely supporting them from a distance, we could easily take a vacation to our neighbouring countries and actively take part in overseas community initiatives while squeezing in a day or two for sightseeing. 

We adults also have more resources at our disposal, and by pooling all that we can, we can help our children by laying the foundation first – refurbish what is already there so that our children can then focus on building more resources for them. 

In Myanmar, we refurbished the building and the playground, and ensured there was proper electricity. Current and future Rafflesians can perhaps build a resource centre next, or extend the library, anything that they think their peers in Myanmar may need to empower them! Together with our children, we make our community projects sustainable.’

The 2014 Plan

In fact, the parents are so enthusiastic about improving the conditions of schools in various communities around the region that they have already started formulating their 2014 plan even before they took off to Myanmar. One of the RPA members, Mr Lal Gunasinghe, together with Mr Chua Wah Ann, has already scouted the villages of Sri Lanka to find a government school that fits the RPA and RPA alumni member’s criteria. In one of the villages that they’ve shortlisted and are potentially confirming, they were intrigued to learn that the parent-teacher relationship is as strong as that of RPA and RI. 

To make each experience unique, the team of parents is also looking into how to ensure that their next community initiative in Sri Lanka isn’t a clone of what has already been achieved in Yangon Girls’ Training School. Besides wanting to strengthen the close relationship between the school and the parents, the team is also thinking of involving their own children and their peers who are Rafflesian alumni and are currently medical undergraduates to provide simple medical checkups in the village.

‘RPA and RPA Alumni would like to progress together with RI, and our community initiative is just one of the many ways we can align ourselves with the school easily. We’d like to welcome parents of alumni to actively participate in RPA’s activities and to continue to be a part of RPA Alumni. At the end of the day, we aren’t just parents of children who have graduated, but a group of friends with similar aspirations for our children and the community at large,’ shared Mr Chua during the recent Myanmar debrief.

The debrief, chaired by Mr Chua Wah Ann, allowed the team of 33 to review their first community initiative and plan for the next one in Sri Lanka.
